## Overview

The Loch Ness Monster, popularly known as Nessie, is a large aquatic cryptid reported in Loch Ness, a deep freshwater loch on the Great Glen Fault in the Scottish Highlands. The modern phenomenon began in 1933 after a new road, the A82, was built along the loch's northern shore and opened up clear views of the water. Witnesses typically describe a long-necked, dark-coloured animal with one or more humps, sometimes interpreted as a relict plesiosaur. A succession of investigations — culminating in the Loch Ness Phenomena Investigation Bureau (1962–1972), Robert Rines's Academy of Applied Science underwater work in the 1970s, the 1987 sonar sweep Operation Deepscan, and the 2018–2019 environmental DNA survey led by Neil Gemmell of the University of Otago — has consistently failed to recover any large unknown vertebrate. The eDNA survey detected no reptile DNA but a notably strong eel signal. The scientific consensus treats Nessie as a folkloric phenomenon explained by misidentification of waves, boat wakes, otters, deer, large eels, and other mundane phenomena, alongside a long history of confirmed hoaxes. The legend supports a substantial Highland tourism economy and remains internationally recognisable.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: The English phrase 'Loch Ness Monster' was coined in May 1933 by Inverness Courier editor Evan Barron, in instructions to reporter Alex Campbell to use that wording in coverage of the Mackay sighting. The diminutive 'Nessie' followed in popular usage.
- **Meaning**: Loch Ness, the lake; 'monster' in the popular cryptid sense.
- **First use**: 1933 (Inverness Courier, Scotland)

## Notable Sightings

### Mackay sighting ⭐
> **Date**: 1933-04-15  
> **Location**: Loch Ness, near Abriachan  
> **Witnesses**: 2 (Hotelkeepers (John and Aldie Mackay))  
> **Evidence**: Inverness Courier coverage  
John and Aldie Mackay, returning home along the new A82, reported seeing a huge 'whale-like' creature roiling the surface of the loch. The story was written up by part-time Inverness Courier reporter Alex Campbell on instructions from editor Evan Barron, who coined the phrase 'monster' in the headline.
**Credibility**: medium — Foundational sighting of the modern Nessie phenomenon.
**Sources**: Inverness Courier (1933-05-02)

### Spicer land crossing ⭐
> **Date**: 1933-07-22  
> **Location**: A82 road near Whitefield, between Dores and Foyers  
> **Witnesses**: 2 (Holidaymakers (George and Mrs. Spicer))  
> **Evidence**: Inverness Courier and Daily Mail coverage  
London company director George Spicer and his wife reported that a long-necked, ungainly creature, perhaps 25 feet long, crossed the A82 in front of their car and disappeared into the loch. Spicer described a small head on a long neck and a thick body but no visible legs.
**Credibility**: medium — Established the long-necked plesiosaur-like image of Nessie.
**Sources**: Inverness Courier (1933-08-04)

---

### Surgeon's Photograph ⭐
> **Date**: 1934-04-19  
> **Location**: Loch Ness, north shore  
> **Witnesses**: 1 (London gynaecologist (Robert Kenneth Wilson))  
> **Evidence**: Original photographic plate; 1994 Spurling deathbed statement  
Wilson submitted to the Daily Mail a photograph of what appeared to be a long-necked aquatic creature. The image became the defining popular icon of Nessie. In 1994 Christian Spurling, the stepson of big-game hunter Marmaduke Wetherell, told researchers David Martin and Alastair Boyd that he had constructed the 'monster' from a toy submarine and modelling clay as part of Wetherell's revenge on the Daily Mail for an earlier hoax exposure.
**Credibility**: low — The textbook example of a long-running cryptid hoax.
**Sources**: Daily Mail (1934-04-21); Boyd, A. & Martin, D. (1999), Nessie: The Surgeon's Photograph Exposed

---

### Dinsdale film ⭐
> **Date**: 1960-04-23  
> **Location**: Loch Ness near Foyers  
> **Witnesses**: 1 (Aeronautical engineer (Tim Dinsdale))  
> **Evidence**: 16 mm film; JARIC analysis report (1965)  
Tim Dinsdale filmed approximately four minutes of 16 mm footage showing a dark hump moving across the loch and submerging. The footage was later analysed by the Joint Air Reconnaissance Intelligence Centre (JARIC) of the RAF, which reported the object as 'probably an animate object' moving at roughly 10 mph.
**Credibility**: medium — One of the most-studied pieces of Nessie film evidence.
**Sources**: Dinsdale, T. (1961), Loch Ness Monster; JARIC report (1965)

---

### Rines underwater 'flipper' photographs ⭐
> **Date**: 1972-08-08  
> **Location**: Urquhart Bay, Loch Ness  
> **Witnesses**: 0 (Underwater camera with synchronized sonar (Academy of Applied Science))  
> **Evidence**: Original photographic frames; Synchronized sonar trace  
Robert Rines's Academy of Applied Science deployed a strobe-lit underwater camera coupled to a sonar in Urquhart Bay. Sonar contacts in early August 1972 coincided with frames apparently showing a flipper-like appendage, on the basis of which Rines and Peter Scott proposed the binomial Nessiteras rhombopteryx.
**Credibility**: low — A central Nessie photograph; later analyses are widely sceptical of the appendage interpretation.
**Sources**: Rines, R. et al. (1976), 'Naming the Loch Ness Monster', Technology Review

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Kelpie / water-horse continuity** _(Ronald Binns, Daniel Loxton)_

Hypothesis that Nessie is a modern reframing of long-standing Highland water-horse and kelpie folklore rather than a biological animal.

### Scientific

**Relict plesiosaur** _(Tim Dinsdale, Robert Rines (early career))_

Hypothesis that Nessie is a surviving population of plesiosaurs trapped in the loch since the Mesozoic or post-glacial era.

**Giant European eel** _(Neil Gemmell, Roy Mackal (early formulation))_

Hypothesis that some Nessie reports are explained by unusually large specimens of the European eel (Anguilla anguilla) resident in the loch.

**Resident sturgeon or large catfish** _(Adrian Shine (earlier formulations))_

Earlier hypothesis attributing some sightings to occasional sturgeon or catfish in the loch.

### Skeptical

**Misidentification of mundane phenomena** _(Maurice Burton, Ronald Binns, Adrian Shine)_

Most Nessie reports are explained by waves, boat wakes, seiches, mirages, swimming deer, otters, water birds, and floating logs.

**Hoaxes and media construction** _(Ronald Binns, Daniel Loxton, Donald Prothero)_

The phenomenon is sustained by deliberate hoaxes (Surgeon's Photograph, Wetherell hippopotamus-foot tracks) and by a press culture that rewards Nessie reports.
